# Code of Conduct

Orison is built and used by a wide range of people. We want every
contributor, reviewer, user, and bystander to feel welcome and to be
able to participate without fear of harassment or unfair treatment.

This document sets the expectations and the process for handling
violations. It applies to every interaction in project spaces — issues,
pull requests, discussion threads, chat channels, conferences, social
media interactions about the project, and private correspondence between
contributors acting in a project capacity.

---

## 1. Our standards

We expect everyone in the project to:

- **Be respectful.** Disagree with ideas, not with people. Critique
  patches, not the patch author. Assume good faith on the part of others
  even when you disagree with their conclusion.
- **Welcome newcomers.** Answer beginner questions patiently; ask
  clarifying questions before assuming someone is making a mistake; link
  to documentation rather than gatekeeping it.
- **Communicate clearly and honestly.** Say what you mean. Surface
  blockers early. Admit when you are wrong; do not double down to save
  face. Quote and link sources rather than paraphrasing in ways that
  change meaning.
- **Respect boundaries.** Do not contact people in private about
  project matters they declined to discuss in public. Honour requests
  to step back from a thread.
- **Centre the work.** Disagreements should be resolved by appealing to
  technical merit, project goals, or documented policy — not by appeals
  to authority, seniority, or popularity.

We will not tolerate:

- Personal attacks, insults, or derogatory language directed at any
  contributor or group.
- Harassment of any kind, including unwanted sexual attention, stalking,
  publication of private information without consent, or sustained
  unwelcome contact.
- Threats of violence or incitement of violence.
- Discrimination based on personal characteristics — including but not
  limited to race, ethnicity, national origin, religion, gender
  identity or expression, sexual orientation, disability, age,
  socioeconomic status, or physical appearance.
- Deliberate misrepresentation of someone else's words or work.
- Sustained disruption of discussions or events.
- Retaliation against anyone who reports a violation in good faith.

This list is not exhaustive. The maintainer team will use judgement when
applying the standards above.

---

## 2. Reporting a violation

If you experience or witness a violation, please report it to the
maintainer team at **conduct@orison-lang.org** (placeholder — see the
current address in [MAINTAINERS.md](./MAINTAINERS.md)).

Reports are received and reviewed by the maintainer team. A report
should include:

- What happened.
- Where it happened (link, channel, event).
- Who was involved (names, handles, or pseudonyms — whatever you have).
- Any supporting material (screenshots, logs, links).
- What outcome you are looking for (a conversation, a written warning, a
  removal, a public response, etc.). You do not have to know the answer
  to this; it helps if you do.

Reports are treated as confidential. The maintainer team will not
publish the reporter's identity without explicit consent. If a maintainer
is the subject of a report, that maintainer is recused from handling the
report.

---

## 3. Response process

After a report is received:

1. **Acknowledgement** — within 48 hours, the recused maintainer team
   acknowledges receipt to the reporter.
2. **Investigation** — the team reviews the report and any supporting
   material. They may reach out to the reporter, the reported person,
   and any witnesses for additional context. The investigation aims to
   conclude within 14 days; the reporter is updated if it will take
   longer.
3. **Decision** — the team agrees on a response (see §4) and informs
   the reporter and (where appropriate) the reported person.
4. **Follow-up** — the team checks in with the reporter to confirm the
   outcome addressed the concern.

The reporter may at any point ask for the report to be closed without
action; the team will honour that request unless the conduct poses an
ongoing safety risk to others in the community.

---

## 4. Possible responses

Depending on the severity and pattern of the behaviour, the maintainer
team may take any of the following actions:

| Action | When it applies |
|---|---|
| **Private clarification.** A maintainer reaches out privately to clarify expectations. | First-time, low-impact issue where the person likely did not realise the behaviour was unwelcome. |
| **Public correction.** The maintainer team posts a moderating note in the thread, asks the parties to reset, and may lock the thread temporarily. | Behaviour was visible to others and a public marker of the standards is useful. |
| **Written warning.** A formal warning is recorded internally and sent to the person. Further violations may escalate. | Repeated low-impact issues, or a single moderate-impact issue. |
| **Temporary suspension.** The person is removed from project spaces for a defined period. | A serious violation or a pattern of repeated violations. |
| **Permanent removal.** The person is permanently banned from project spaces. | A severe violation, repeated escalating behaviour, or retaliation against a reporter. |

Actions above a written warning require a supermajority of the maintainer
team (see [GOVERNANCE.md §2.3](./GOVERNANCE.md)). The reported person
will be informed of the action and the reason in writing.

A person who has received an enforcement action may appeal in writing
to the maintainer team within 30 days. The appeal is reviewed by a
maintainer who was not involved in the original decision, with the rest
of the team consulted as needed.

---

## 5. Scope

This code applies to behaviour in:

- The project repositories on GitHub (issues, PRs, discussions, wiki).
- Official project communication channels (mailing list, chat, forum).
- Conference and meetup spaces where someone is representing the project.
- Direct messages and private correspondence about project matters.

It also applies to behaviour outside project spaces that has a direct
impact on the safety or well-being of project participants — for
example, harassment of a contributor on social media because of their
project work.
